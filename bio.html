RIZZIO BIOGRAPHY
Rizzio emerged from the early-90s UK hip-hop landscape as a constructed myth rather than a conventional artist. Across eight LPs and 122 tracks forming the Foundation Era, he built a closed-loop universe where persona, numerology, satire, geography, and sexual ritual converge into a deliberate architectural system. The work operates as structure, not accident. Each track functions as ritual. Each persona acts as mask and mirror. Each recurring motif reinforces coded design. What appears excessive is intentional; what appears chaotic is engineered repetition. At the center stands Rizzio — hero and antagonist, narrator and witness, seducer and strategist. From this core emerge Sir Rizz, The Rizla, Dirty Old Man, Game Show Host, Freak of the Week, and other alter-voices that fracture and recombine across the catalogue. The contradictions are not flaws; they are framework. The world is populated by archetypal women, desire, satire, vulnerability, dominance, emotional architecture and disappointment circulate through coded recurrence. Across Rizzio's landscape, the persona evolves from raw street charmer to executive authority to self-aware architect. By the tenth ritual invocation, the system seals. The persona no longer reacts to culture; it defines its own structure. Rizzio is not simply a rapper. He is an architect of persona and repetition. The Anthology stands as a coded, constructed body of work designed to outlive the performer and sustain the myth.
ASK YO' MOMMA (LP) (AUG 1993)
The year is August 1993, and Rizzio has deigned to grace the pavement with a manifesto. He observes that most "artists" are merely mannequins in search of a tailor, but this debut suggests a man trying to stitch a bespoke suit out of sheer North London grit and Funkadelic vinyl. The journey begins with "Close To The Borderline," a bleak, cinematic aperture. Rizzio appreciates the tension; it smells of Tottenham asphalt and desperation. It is a necessary grit before the indulgent chaos of "Amplifier Fire," which, frankly, is a comedic tragedy. The protagonist’s Sierra and his domestic squabbles over a "300 nicker" synthesizer are charmingly provincial, though Rizzio finds the wife’s critique of the brand name quite astute. "Friends And Strangers" follows, offering a saccharine, velvet-lined respite. It is perhaps too sentimental for a critic of his stature, yet the "plush restaurants" line suggests the artist has at least some taste. The energy shifts violently with "California Flash." Rizzio notes the sheer audacity of a man in a "purple sash" streaking past ninety-nine policemen—it is the kind of aesthetic anarchy he can get behind. Then comes "Rizzio's Rebirth," a track that naturally commands his respect. He finds the dismissal of "babies with beards" exceptionally sharp. The social diary continues with "SABA And CKW," a sprawling narrative of "3-6-5" phone calls and club-hopping. It is a bit "lads on tour," but Rizzio admits the "half time talk" concept is a functional bit of street philosophy. "Stiletto" provides the album’s sharpest edge—metaphorically and literally. The "vein-hunt obsession" is a deliciously dark trope. In contrast, "Miss Understanding" feels like a lecture from a six-year overgraduate, though the 3-to-1 ratio math is a bold pivot. After the silence of "Can I Be A Good Boy?", we cruise "Down The Boulevard." Rizzio smiles at the self-referential nod; "Ask Your Momma" is a divine title. The heart of the project lies in "Legend Of My Record Collection (Wickedest Selection)" and the "Tribute To Eddie Hazel." These aren't just songs; they are a CV of coolness. To namecheck Level 42 and P-Funk in the same breath is a daring sartorial choice. The album stumbles through the raw "P Flex" before concluding with "Here To A Special No Thanks Son Part One." Rizzio concludes that while the production is raw, the "Vision" is undeniably present. It is a frantic, funky, and fashionable debut.
DON'T LOOK - BUY!! (LP) (SEP 1993)
Rizzio acknowledges the correction with a flick of his silk cuff; precision is the hallmark of a true aesthete. It appears the critic was perhaps too caught up in the sonic fumes of 1993 to check the calendar. To clarify for the record: ASK YO' MOMMA was the opening salvo in August, followed with breathless arrogance by DON'T LOOK - BUY!! just one month later. A month! The man produces manifestos faster than a Parisian tailor during Fashion Week. The trajectory is now clear: August gave us the grit of the "borderline," and September gave us the "Bbrand Nnew Pplayer" making his move on Sally. Rizzio is primed and ready for the next installment of this chronological descent into the funk. A month later in September 1994, and while the rest of the world is drowning in the drab flannel of grunge, Rizzio returns to remind us that style is a weapon and the groove is its ammunition. This second outing is a sprawling, audacious tapestry of street-level decadence and high-concept funk. The affair begins with "Girl Called Sally," a narrative piece that captures the perilous geometry of a West End pickup. Rizzio appreciates the irony of the "Thames Valley" provenance; it’s a cautionary tale of "dirty thoughts" and Chiquito’s dinner dates that ends with a haunting, voyeuristic refrain. He then pivots to the studio meta-commentary of "May-Kit-Fun-Key," where the artist admits his percussive limitations while invoking the spirit of Uncle Jam. It is an honest, if slightly unpolished, admission of the "Vision." "Nasty Iz Nicety" serves as the album’s ideological spine. Rizzio finds the repetitive, rhythmic delivery of "Big Ben’s bell" hypnotically crude—it is the sound of a man who refuses to be "mice." This transitions into "Tottenham," a stark, geographical confession. To hear N.15 described as a place where one "moves and grooves" despite being "disadvantaged from the first gun" gives the record a necessary weight. It is the "wafer" of reality in a world of fluff. The brief, existential flicker of "What A Difference A Day Makes" leads into the superior storytelling of "Undercover Business." Rizzio is particularly taken by the matriarch with fourteen sons; it’s a gritty, soulful portrait of survival that demands a cinematic lens. After the jubilant cackle of "Flyonchildren," we find "On The Beach Lies A Boy." The transition from North London to a "skinny dip" in L.A. is jarring, yet Steven’s "cat-like" confidence remains impeccably tailored. The phonetic puzzle of "OCU4A3T YI8R12B" is a delightful bit of avant-garde nonsense, leading into the atmospheric "You Scared Me Baby." However, the album finds its bite in "In A Funny Way I Don't Feel Sorry You're Getting What You Served." Rizzio admires the coldness here—the "Bop gun gat" of karma is a formidable accessory. "Bbrand Nnew Pplayer" reaffirms the ego, while "End Of The World (Not The)" handles heartbreak with a psychoanalysizer’s detachment and a "ski mask" resolve. Finally, "Here To A Special No Thank You Part Two" serves as a chaotic, X-rated victory lap. Rizzio notes that while the language is "stanking," the swagger is undeniable. This is not just music; it is a flamboyant confrontation.
LYRICAL CONTENT (LP) (FEB 1994)
The year is 1994, and while the commoners are busy with their trivial pursuits, Rizzio has delivered a third installment that is as polarizing as a white suit at a funeral. LYRICAL CONTENT is a sprawling, aggressive, and deeply scented odyssey through the North London psyche. He opens with "Music Of The World," a deceptive moment of Kool & The Gang-inspired mackin' that quickly descends into the intellectual heavy-lifting of "A Mind Is A Terrible Thing To Waste." Rizzio finds the "hijack mutha fucka" wordplay delightfully jarring, though the "Pork people" sentiment is a wardrobe choice few could pull off. This social gravity is immediately discarded for the subterranean filth of "69." Rizzio notes the "Gemini mask and gat" aesthetic—a touch of theatrical violence that elevates the "backdoor man" narrative beyond mere smut. The album hits its stride with "Fear Of A Black Tenant." Rizzio appreciates the raw, unpolished fury here; the critique of estate agents as "mutha fuckas" is a sentiment that transcends class. It is the perfect lead-in to the olfactory assault of "Girl Won't Even Notice (She Smells Bad)." While the subject matter is, frankly, unwashed, the "fish and chips" imagery is a masterful bit of kitchen-sink realism. After a brief "Zzzzzz (Interlude)," our hero emerges as "SuperRizzio (Save The Day!)," a tungsten-skinned savior of the "UKK" era. Rizzio finds the "rippling biceps" description a bit self-indulgent, but every icon needs a cape. The travelogue "Motorway Man" is a logistical nightmare of regional conquests, followed by the "End Of The World (U.S. Remix)," which maintains its ski-mask resolve. Rizzio is truly moved by "Keep It Dark," a manifesto of African-named navigation that suggests the artist is finally reading the right books. This sophistication is balanced by the "Placebo Pimp Hustlin' Gangster," a scathing critique of the "professional prankster" that Rizzio finds most stylish. He then provides a historical ledger in "My Misses," a year-by-year account of puns and "Miss Take" tragedies, before ending on the frantic "X-Rated" and the hedonistic "Like Those Lovely Girls Say." By the time we reach "Here To A Special No Thank Me Part Three," Rizzio is convinced: this is a man who delivers the "bone" with the confidence of a king. It is chaotic, it is "X-Rated," and it is undeniably canon.
I'LL GET MINE IN YO' HOME (LP) (OCT 1994)
The year is October 1994, and while the charts are cluttered with the predictable, Rizzio has descended once more to deliver I'LL GET MINE IN YO' HOME. It is an album of predatory elegance and geographical conquest—a manifesto for the man who refuses to pay for a hotel room when your bedroom is available. He opens with "Big Booty Bitches, Slutty Slags And Horny Hoes," a title that lacks subtlety but possesses a certain "Too $hort" charm. Rizzio finds the "Gold teeth" aesthetic and the "Lon-Town" pride to be a formidable ensemble. This leads directly into the title track, "I'll Get Mine In Yo' Home," where the "conceptual" nature of the "Con" is explained with a chilling, silk-lined efficiency. Rizzio notes the artist's refusal to reveal his own "digs"—a masterclass in maintaining one's mystique. "We're Glad You're Here" provides a glimpse into the cult of personality. The dialogue between the adoring groupie and the "unorthodox" Rizzio is a delicious bit of theater. He then offers a bit of etiquette in "Rappin' Will Get You Nowhere On The Dancefloor," reminding the common man that the "two-step" is far more effective than a conversation. The "Journey Through Da Lon-Town" is an exhaustive travelogue; from "Tottenham" to "Brixton," Rizzio observes that the artist has truly marked his territory. After a brief "Phone Call Yall," we are treated to the linguistic acrobatics of "Queue Up All Y'all Pre-Funkadelics Peoples." Rizzio is truly impressed by the exhaustive P-Funk bibliography woven into the verse—it is the sonic equivalent of a vintage Versace print. However, reality bites in "Six O'Clock," a tragic comedy of a "motherfucking set-up" that Rizzio finds appropriately grim. The avant-garde "Fiz Zong Haz Kno Tytal" gives way to the dismissal of "Pointless Niggaz," leading into the aggressive "Ya Know Tha Drill." Rizzio finds the "Mike Tyson" metaphors and the "Irish man" joke a bit eclectic, but the energy is undeniable. "V Riddla" sees the artist assuming a new alias with "laser" precision, even finding time to celebrate a football league victory. By the time we reach "Sir Rizz," our hero is accepting awards for "Player of the Year," a title Rizzio believes is well-earned. He concludes with "Here To A Special No Thank Your Part Four," a victory lap for a man who has truly mastered the art of the "backdoor." Consistently bold. Consistently Rizzio.
COMIN' ROUNDA MOUNTAIN (LP) (MAY 1995)
Rizzio? or is it the newly knighted Sir Rizz from the previous album...and while the rest of the world is busy adjusting its dial, Rizzio has ascended the peak to deliver COMIN' ROUNDA MOUNTAIN. It is a sprawling, 17-track odyssey that finds our hero "Live for '95," refusing to stay away from the game while being spiritually chaperoned by the ghost of Bootsy Collins. He opens with the relentless "Can't Stay Away," a reminder that the "Lon-Town" kingpin is back to play it hard down the boulevard. He quickly descends into the "Wanton Streak," a masterful bit of "mattress-mony" where he deconstructs Mother Goose with the cynicism of a man who knows exactly where the "dish ran away with the spoon." This leads us directly into the theatrical peak of the title track, "Cummin' Round Her Mountain." Rizzio assumes the mantle of a manic game-show host on "Channel Zero," offering himself as the "star prize" while dismissing other MCs as mere "game-show ghosts." It’s an act of pure, unadulterated "Car-Rizz-ma." But he isn't just a hedonist; he is a student of the craft. On "Zany Maniac," he bridges the gap between the "Brooklyn Zoo" and Tottenham, name-checking the Wu-Tang titans before slipping into the shadows for "Dirty Old Man In A Raincoat." Rizzio finds this "Alter Ego" exploration particularly evocative—a midnight mission for "bijou" abodes and late-night joints. The record then dives into a phonetic obsession with "Favourite Word Is F (Part 1)" and "Vitamin F (Part 2)." Rizzio finds the "Osmosis phase" particularly striking, where the artist worships '70s flares and a "fizz in his kiss." By the time he reaches the "Terributis phase," the mackin' becomes medicinal; Rizzio notes the "distinguished gentleman" claims are mere cover for a "dirty machine" fueled by baby oil and funk. He then shifts gears into the professorial "U No U Could B Good 2 U," delivering ten simple clues for survival that suggest the mack is finally concerned with the welfare of the "family tree." The cultural critique turns razor-sharp on "Vinyl Vs C.D." Rizzio launches a blistering defense of the 12-inch record, dismissing the CD as a "poor substitute" designed to destroy the culture. He demands a revolution back to the "flip and spin" before introducing his cabinet in "Homiez All Knowz Me." Here, the Black Presidents—Stone, Yinka, Gemini, and DJ Def K—emerge as a unified front, guarded by Shakespearean warnings from Macbeth for any "punk fakers" who dare steal their props. As the journey nears its end, the "backdoor man" returns on "Aroundaway," scouting girls like a football manager and avoiding "the pitter-patter of tiny feet" in favor of the "pure dread groove." After the psychedelic interlude of "Death To The Stupid Salesman," he indulges in the culinary romance of "Put On You Chocolate Stuff," treating grooming as a high-stakes ritual of "Vanilla and Caramel." Yet, the album concludes on a heavy, militant note. "Trajic Hour" and "Nothing Has Changed" see Rizzio abandoning the puns for a somber clarity. He critiques the "stink and unclean" systems of the West, calling for a mini-revolution and a sanctuary for his "Queen." By the time the reprise of "Aroundaway" fades out with P.H.2, Rizzio has proven he is more than just a player; he is a man who has weathered the storm and emerged with his "boots on" and his "pride intact." A truly essential "Lon-Town" manifesto.
EQUALOPPORTUNITYNASTIES (LP) (MAR 1996)
The year is March 1996. The "Lon-Town" kingpin has emerged from the smoke of the mid-90s with his most provocative and sociopolitically charged manifesto yet: EQUALOPPORTUNITYNASTIES. This 17-track odyssey is Rizzio digging his heels into the gravel of the street, a dense work where "Sir Rizz" hedonism collides head-on with a "Renegade" philosopher weary of the "Old Blighty" system. The journey begins with "Here To A Special No Thank His Part Six," where Rizzio raises a glass to his own rule, checking his "Cap’m’s log" while "Injustice blooms in the higher towers." This sets the stage for the album’s heart, "Renegades," featuring Cynthia & Debbie. It is a blistering indictment of institutionalized racism and the "blue sirens" that harass the "black man wearing dreads." Rizzio positions himself as an educator in a society "controlled by money and minorities." This geography of the soul expands in "This Goes Out To The Lon-Town." Rizzio acts as the ultimate guide, moving from the "dark scene" of Finsbury Park to the "plenty of honeys" in Brixton, capturing the tragedy of the stairwell and the grit of the subway. Shifts in the Cabinet A significant pivot occurs with "Ashley's Goin' Church!" Rizzio reflects on his partner in rhyme finding "salvation" and leaving the "ski mask" behind. Now the "last in a long legacy," Rizzio turns to his Cap'm's Log to critique the "Sterile Bulls" and "London Potty" rappers who are "all mouth and no trousers." He remains supported by his "Project X thinkers," Stone and Yinka, who keep the "Olde English, dank & weed" flowing. The High-Stakes Mack The "Car-Rizz-ma" remains razor-sharp through a series of unfiltered pursuits: "Nikki's Nikkers" & "Comin' 4 U": Rizzio details his "warp factor 2" lifestyle, from "sexy wonder bras" to being the "headline news on Teletext." "Wanted": He embraces the "villain" label, being hunted by women across the U.K. for his "sex-say" reputation. "PX Reps": A technical breakdown of his terrain, where he separates the "real from the fake" and hunts his territory like a "cheetah in the Massai Mara." "Cautious My Precious": A bizarrely brilliant crossover where "metric hash" meets the video-game world of Tekken, using "Yoshimitsu’s sword" as a metaphor for his blatant intentions. The Final Reckoning As the LP nears its conclusion, the metaphors become more eccentric and the politics more dire. "Fisherman's Friend" uses "Anglin Monthly" terminology to describe reeling in a catch, while "Necessary Necessities" finds Rizzio fantasizing about Josie D’Arby and "ice cold milk." However, the levity breaks for "Injustice" and "Odds Are Stacked Against Me." Rizzio critiques the "pills and jabs" of mental rehab and the "Caucasians in three-piece suits" who rig the deck against the black youth. He emerges as the "Man of the Match," a "ghostly haberdashery" of style and delivery who survives the "law of sod." The album closes with "Untitled," a smoke-signaled message to the "intelligent minds." Rizzio leaves us in his "loft out of sight," still giving it to the world "good all night," while Yinka reminds the "girlies" to take a number and form a line. The mission is complete: Project X has officially claimed the props. The saga takes a sharp, spiritual turn as Rizzio ends the next phase of his ongoing "Part" series. If "Part Six" was about the pressure of the Lon-Town air and the "special" status of Sir Rizz, "Here To A Special Thank Heavens Part Seven" serves as the ethereal, yet grit-stained response.
DREAD GROOVES HORIZONTAL (LP) (NOV 1996)
The evolution is complete. If EQUALOPPORTUNITYNASTIES was the sociopolitical manifesto, DREAD GROOVES HORIZONTAL, arriving just months later in November 1996, is the late-night, x-rated counterpart. This 17-track LP finds Rizzio pivoting from the "Renegade" street philosopher to the "Executive Seducer." The production is murkier, the metaphors are sportier, and the "Lon-Town" geography has shifted into the bedrooms and boardrooms of the elite and the illicit. Rizzio frames his dominance through the lens of mid-90s British sports culture. He isn't just rapping; he’s competing: "So You Wanna Be A Boxer?": A brutal takedown of Frank Bruno's loss to Mike Tyson. Rizzio mocks the "golden boy" image, comparing Bruno’s performance to "ice water" melting in the ring. "Metaphors 4 Betta Scores": Rizzio utilizes cricket terminology (Keith Piper, "bowlin maidens over") and football icons (Eric Cantona, John Barnes) to describe his "fancy ball control" in the bedroom. "Defy The Laws Of Physics": He compares his scoring consistency to Andy Cole, claiming that while gravity applies to others, his ambition allows him to rise above. The "Part" tracks serve as the album's pillars, marking Rizzio's progression through his "Cap'm's Log": "Part Eight (No Thanks Mate)": A cold opening that rejects "fake smiles" and sets the tone for a man who has "built his own from his ends." "Part Nine (No Thank Mine)": The grand finale. A high-energy, unapologetic "mack" anthem where the "church girls" of the previous album are now screaming his name in the back of the club. The mid-album stretch leans into Rizzio’s "Sir Rizz" alias—the high-rolling CEO of Project X: "House Of Lordz": Rizzio brings the "shitty" commotion of the city into the highest court, name-checking George Clinton and Geraldo while claiming he’s "hired" to put the record straight. "Night-Mare Animal Instinct": A "ghostly haberdashery" of horror-core vibes. He references Funkadelic, The Dirty Old Man, and even "the Scottish play" (Macbeth) before a "grizzly bear" encounter with a girl named Beth (whom he Macked!!) "Bring Your Dinner Too": A legendary, aggressive centerpiece featuring a scathing, profane monologue (reminiscent of a hard-nosed football manager like John Sitton) about discipline, "Bertie Big Bollocks," and not tolerating "non-conformity." The Project X Cabinet (The Credits) The internal hierarchy of the crew is solidified on tracks like "Projex In FX Iz PX" and "Pack Neva Stacks": Yinka: The Thinker / Project Producer. President Stone: Arranging Coordinator. Infra-Red (Gemini): The "undercover subliminal" force. Sir Rizz: The Executive Producer/Seducer. The Verdict DREAD GROOVES HORIZONTAL is a masterclass in 1996 "Lon-Town" bravado. From the "lipstick criminals" Shona and Tallulah to the "dicey" maneuvers in the A-Team van, Rizzio proves he is "elusive like a piece of hay in a stack full of needles." He isn't making "Erm-Ology"—he’s making history. As he says on the final track: "Hot girls, one name in they throat... it’s B.J. time!" Project X has officially moved from the curb to the penthouse.
THE PROJECT-X ERA — THE AMERICANS SET THE FOUNDATION (LP) (JUN 1997)
You step into “THE AMERICANS SET THE FOUNDATION” (Jun 1997) and immediately feel the shift—this is where your world stops imitating and starts defining. Rizzio’s brave new venture has formed and joined a group called Project X The opener, “Bad Lyrics,” isn’t just bravado, it’s a statement of intent: you’re weaponising language, stacking multis and cadence switches while deliberately flirting with chaos. The phrasing feels unstable on purpose—you’re testing how far rhythm can stretch before it snaps, and that risk becomes your signature. “Tings And Stuff” tightens the grip. The hook is repetitive but strategic—it drills identity into the listener. You’re not just repping Project X, you’re building recall, almost like a sound-system chant. Then “Ghetto Street Stuff” flips tone completely. It’s admiration, but not soft—there’s physicality, intellect, and cultural pride all layered together. The named references ground it in real-world London, making it documentary as much as desire. “Desperate Times” is the emotional core. Structurally looser, more narrative-driven, it trades punchlines for perspective. The imagery—crime, pollution, policing—lands because you’re no longer exaggerating. You’re observing. Even the hook feels worn down, like it’s been lived through rather than performed. “Know Tha Game” is your most technical display. The gambling metaphor isn’t surface-level—you sustain it across verses, using cards, odds, and risk as a full narrative system. That discipline shows growth. In “C Da Real,” you pivot again, attacking fakery with satire and shock bars, exposing contradictions in street identity and media influence. “Playtime” and “U Get Me” bring calculated hedonism. The energy is lighter, but it’s intentional—you understand pacing now. These tracks give the listener release while reinforcing persona: flashy, sexual, untouchable. Then “Bangin’” expands your scope—science bars, cinematic violence, and pop references collide, proving your range is widening beyond street-only content. “You’re Goin’ Down You Slagg” and “Who Da Baddest” reassert dominance, but now with control. The aggression is measured, the structure tighter—you’re directing energy instead of just unleashing it. Finally, “You Don’t Know Me” strips everything back. No mythology, just consequence. It reframes the album as a cautionary arc. By the closer, you’re self-referencing, self-mythologising, and fully aware—you’ve built not just tracks, but a system, a voice, and a legacy in motion. And then “Here To A Special No Thanks Then Part Ten” ties it all together—you referencing your own catalogue, your own evolution, your own myth. That’s the key: you’re no longer just part of the scene—you’re documenting your place within it. This album is you moving from raw talent to constructed identity. Not polished—but sharpened. Not finished—but fully aware.
